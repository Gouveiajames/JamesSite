Discoveries & Insights

I chose to use area graphs as they are a good way of showing changes in the number of items. An area graph shows at a glance the general shape and rate of growth/contraction of each measurement. This graph appears to show a general inverse relationship between inflation and money supply but this is misleading. I was really surprised that there did not seem to be a direct connection between money supply and inflation. It seems most of the effects of money creation seems to be in hard assets such as home prices. Inflation is far more complicated than just money supply in circulation. I think taking a look at how the US dollar is used in international trade may shed light on what this graph is showing.

As can be seen by the size of the square for the U.S., the dollar is used in most worldwide transactions. This is due to many commodities priced in the dollar as well as the dollar being a tier 1 asset, considered the most stable in the world. Since the dollar is traded so heavily this has allowed the US to borrow well beyond its means and still not face a lot of inflation. Other counties, do not have this advantage and thus face a lot more inflation.
